The African American Experience in Boyle County

“We Were Here”

After the Civil War, sixteen Black communities flourished across Boyle County. A century later, urban renewal erased much of their physical world, but not their story. This archive preserves it in the voices of the people who lived it.

What is a place?

The stories and experiences reflected in We Were Here are connected by a powerfully central theme - the significance of having a “sense of place.”

“As normally experienced, sense of place quite simply is, as natural and straightforward as our fondness for certain colors and culinary tastes, and the thought that it might be complicated, or even very interesting, seldom crosses our minds. Until, as sometimes happens, we are deprived of these attachments and find ourselves adrift, literally dislocated, in unfamiliar surroundings we do not comprehend and care for even less. On these unnerving occasions, sense of place may assert itself in pressing and powerful ways, and its often subtle components—as subtle, perhaps, as absent smells in the air or not enough visible sky—come surging into awareness. It is then we come to see that attachments to places may be nothing less than profound, and that when these attachments are threatened we may feel threatened as well. Places, we realize, are as much a part of us as we are part of them, and senses of place—yours, mine, and everyone else's—partake complexly of both.”

Keith Basso, Wisdom Sits in Places, xiii-xiv
