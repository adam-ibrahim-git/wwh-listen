The full scholarly history behind this archive: how urban renewal reshaped Danville’s Black neighborhoods between 1960 and the mid-1980s.

The “urban renewal” period in the United States began with the passing of the Housing Act of 1949 and was further entrenched with the Housing Act of 1954. Both acts allowed local housing authorities to identify “slums and blighted areas” for “clearance” and then “redevelopment” using federal funds (Biles 2011). Between 1949 and the mid-1970s, when funding for urban renewal projects dried up, somewhere between 2,000 and 3,000 such projects were carried out in more than 1,000 municipalities in the United States, to the tune of $13 billion in federal dollars given out to local municipalities (Fullilove n.d., Fullilove 2016, University of Richmond Digital Scholarship Lab). Comprehensive demographic statistics on those displaced by these projects were only kept until 1966, but even with these truncated statistics, the scale of the program was demonstrably skewed in terms of race. More than six hundred municipalities in the United States displaced families, and of the minimum of the one-third of a million families displaced, the large majority of these families were of color. In many locations, including large cities like Philadelphia, more than two-thirds of the families were of color (University of Richmond Digital Scholarship Lab n.d.).

In Danville, Kentucky, the urban renewal period was a microcosm of these larger structural forces. According to local historian Mike Denis, six areas of the city in total were affected by urban renewal in a process that stretched from 1960 until at least the mid 1980s (personal communication).

The first of these projects in Danville was directly motivated by Centre College’s interests in expansion and “beautification.” The college had integrated its campus with the Kentucky College for Women in 1962, and had constructed three new dormitories for women on the north side of Main Street (Weston 2019, Centre College Special Collections Digital Archives n.d.). As part of the planning for campus gender integration, Centre proposed annexing the area along North Seventh Street, which ran perpendicular to Main Street, and Rowe Street, which ran parallel to Main Street on its north side. Both areas were inhabited exclusively by Black families, most of whom were renters. The federal government agreed to match Centre’s commitment to spend $180,000 on the project at two-to-one (“Bailey Heard at Breakfast Honoring Centre” 1961); this eventually became a grant of $519,600 by the time the project was greenlit in July of 1964 (&quot;Urban Renewal Project Is Given Green Light&quot; 1964). After the area was cleared of housing (not completed until 1969; &quot;Urban Renewal Buys May Start In Sept.&quot; 1969), this area became a soccer field and recreation area. In the late 20-teens, Centre College built another dorm in this area.

The second urban renewal project, which overlapped substantially in time with the Seventh Street project, removed a Black entrepreneurial district that stretched along the block of South Second Street from Main Street. This area had developed over the course of the 20th century as a hub for Black people and families from Danville and the surrounding Boyle County area and contained, over the years, a succession of restaurants, barber shops, cab companies, and an iconic Black Masonic Lodge (the “Doric Lodge”). The Second Street urban renewal project was first “endorsed” by Danville’s Chamber of Commerce in late 1965(&quot;Retail Merchants 'Endorse in Principle' 2nd Street Urban Renewal Development&quot; 1965), but it wasn’t until 1970 that the full $1.46 million of combined loan and grant money was awarded for the project by the Department of Housing and Urban Development (&quot;Urban Renewal Plan On 2nd Street May Start Immediately&quot; 1970). Demolition began in December of 1970 (&quot;Clearing Urban Renewal Land&quot; 1970), but there were multiple delays because some property owners refused to sell, forcing the Danville Urban Renewal Agency to file multiple rounds of condemnation suits to acquire all of the properties (&quot;Suits Are Filed For Property&quot; 1971; &quot;Take Action To Acquire 4 Properties&quot; 1972). The last of these properties was not acquired until the middle of 1974 (&quot;Last Tract Purchased By Urban Renewal&quot; 1974). Because this project removed Black-owned businesses and Black public space, the “Second Street Project” has become the emotional centerpiece of the urban renewal period for the remaining Black community, representing what the community lost during that time.

For Danville’s Urban Renewal Agency, the goal of the Second Street urban renewal project was to expand John Gill Weisiger Memorial Park, which had been gifted to the Commonwealth of Kentucky by Emma Weisiger in October 1937, and until the urban renewal period occupied only the block between First and Second Streets on the south side of Main Street. In 1942, the Commonwealth erected several replicas of colonial-era buildings that memorialize colonial Danville and the historical narrative that Danville is the “birthplace of Kentucky’s statehood”. These replica buildings include a jail, meetinghouse, and courthouse. In 1951, the Danville & Boyle County Historical Society dedicated a mostly original colonial era post office, the “first post office west of the Alleghenies,” which was relocated from Walnut St., one block to the south. All of these structures still stand today and form the core of what is known locally as Constitution Square Park.

The original Weisiger park was sandwiched between a small Black neighborhood on South First Street and the Second Street entrepreneurial district. The areas that had become majority-Black, however, also included original colonial-era buildings, On First Street, these included Grayson’s Tavern at First and Walnut Streets (the first tavern to open in Danville in the 18th century and the first site of local politicking, circa 1785), the Watts-Bell House residence (circa 1816-17), Danville’s first two-room schoolhouse (circa 1820), and Fisher’s Row (Danville’s first rental properties, circa 1816-17). On Second Street, the one remaining colonial-era building is the Albam Gilpin Goldsmith House (the residence of a celebrated 19th century physician, circa 1825). In the end, the urban renewal project affecting this area removed all of the private residences on First and Second Streets not deemed a “historical building” (“2nd Street Urban Renewal Project Provides Danville with its Greatest Opportunity for Development in 100 Years” 1966).

On First Street, the buildings named above were renovated in the mid-1970s as part of the Second Street urban renewal project. Currently, Fisher’s Row houses Danville’s Economic Development Partnership & Industrial Foundation and the Chamber of Commerce, the schoolhouse is a conference center, and Grayson’s Tavern holds the Boyle County Public Library’s “Story Center.” First Street itself was replaced with a brick walkway, creating continuity with the existing Weisiger Park. The block of South Second Street that had been the Black entrepreneurial district is occupied by what is known as “Governor's Circle” a landscaped walkway with curved brick planters that includes plaques commemorating each of Kentucky's governors, all of whom are white. These plaques are arrayed around a large-scale bronze sculpture of the emblem on Kentucky’s state flag, a true-to-life depiction of a “frontiersman” and a “statesman” clasped in a “uniting” embrace. This sculpture is placed on a pedestal at the center of Governor’s Circle. The one remaining “historical” building on Second Street, the Goldsmith House, houses Danville’s visitor center and a gift shop. This whole block, between what used to be First Street and Second Street (from east to west) and Main St. to Walnut St. (from north to south) is now known as “Constitution Square Historic Site.”

The Second Street urban renewal project was first “endorsed” by Danville’s Chamber of Commerce in late 1965 (“The Retail Merchants Division of the Danville-Boyle County Chamber of Commerce ‘endorse In principle’ a new urban renewal proposal that would impact the Downtown 2nd Street”, 1965), but it wasn’t until 1970 that the full $1.46 million of combined loan and grant money was awarded for the project by the Department of Housing and Urban Development (“Urban Renewal Plan on 2nd Street May Start Immediately” 1970). As a prerequisite to the structure clearance that would take place under urban renewal, 67% of the structures on First and Second Streets had been deemed “substandard” (“Discussion of Urban Renewal Held” 1967). Demolition began in December of 1970 (“Clearing Urban Renewal Land 1970), but there were multiple delays because some property owners refused to sell, forcing the Danville Urban Renewal Agency to file multiple rounds of condemnation suits to acquire all of the properties (“Suits Are Filed For Property” 1971; “Take Action To Acquire 4 Properties 1972). The last of these properties was not acquired until the middle of 1974 (“Last Track Purchased By Urban Renewal” 1974).

The Trustees of the Doric Lodge also initially refused to sell (“Doric Masonic Trustees Reject Building Offer” 1971), citing a lack of a fair-market offer (“Letters to the Editor” 1972). The Doric Lodge building was finally acquired by the Urban Renewal Agency in March of 1972 for $34,000 (“Masonic Hall Sold At $34,000” 1972), and was demolished in 1974 (Michael Hughes, personal communication). With it, perhaps the most iconic physical symbol of Black achievement and identity in Danville was removed. The Doric Lodge had been built with Black wealth in the late 19-teens, when ten local Black Masons each put up $1,000 in collateral to secure a loan for the building construction (Mike Denis, n.d.:215). Tellingly, however, in the initial 1966 survey of the state of buildings in the Second Street area, the Doric Lodge had not been identified as a “building substandard to a degree warranting clearance.” However, it was demolished, while Grayson’s Tavern, which had been labeled as “substandard,” was preserved and renovated because it had been identified as a “historical building” (“2nd St. Urban Renewal Project Provides Danville With Its Greatest Opportunity for Development In 100 Years” 1966). Today, the exact site occupied by the Doric Lodge is now taken up by Governor’s Circle.

The federal government collected demographic data on urban renewal projects only until 1966, which covers the period of the Seventh Street project. According to The University of Richmond’s “Renewing Inequality” project, 48 families were relocated from the Seventh Street area over the course of the project, and all of these families identified as Black. For the Second Street project, effected after 1966, data is more circumstantial. According to the renewal project catchment area identified in the Danville Advocate-Messenger (“2nd St. Urban Renewal Project Provides Danville With Its Greatest Opportunity for Development In 100 Years” 1966), 44 structures were identified as single family residential (33 structures) or multi-family residential (11 structures, with a further six labeled “mixed use”). According to local sources, almost all, if not all, families who lived in this area of the city were Black.

Further urban renewal projects, all of which displaced Black people, continued in Danville into the 1980s before petering out. Dozens of Black families, and several historically tight-knit Black neighborhoods were affected in West Danville and South Danville. However, none of these projects were as visible, or as high-profile, as the Seventh and Second Street projects. The later projects focused on the promise of connecting running water and sewer lines to Black homes and thereby allowing for the incorporation of previously unincorporated areas of the city into Danville. For the most part, however, these projects succeeded only in bulldozing existing housing and then running short on funding, instigating lawsuits. These areas of West and South Danville are largely now zoned for commercial use. As local historian Mike Denis has put it, in Danville, ​“urban renewal ended ​with a whimper, not a bang” (personal communication).

In all, the net effect of these projects, as we have heard from person after person in oral history interviews, was the successful dissolution of Black community in the Danville area. While some people were successfully re-housed in town, for example to the housing project called Batewood Homes further south on Second Street, many people simply left Boyle county altogether (Michael Hughes and Charles Grey, personal communication). In at least one documented instance, the initial promise of rehousing and home ownership was thwarted when members of the Urban Renewal Agency reneged on the price of the house to be built, telling the buyer that he would have to take out a second mortgage to purchase the new home (James Hunn, personal communication).

The “We Were Here” Exhibition

During 2022-23 academic year, the first author partnered with DBCAAHS and Centre College’s Norton Center for the Arts, a large regional performing arts center with an annual visitation reaching over 55,000 that bills itself as “Central Kentucky’s epicenter of the performing arts” to curate an exhibition highlighting both the DBCAAHS photo collection and the years of work resulting from our partnership. Each semester, the Norton Center mounts an art installation in its main foyer, which is a large space through which every Norton Center patron has to pass to enter the Center’s principal theater space. In the fall of 2021, the first author and DBCAAHS approached the Norton Center Director with the unusual idea of mounting an exhibition focused on local Black history and Danville’s urban renewal period. The Norton Center had never mounted an installation like this before, as all of its previous exhibitions had focused on fine art. In the words of Steven Hoffman, the Norton Center Director, the moment he was sold on the project was when he realized that our project was “really about storytelling” (Steven Hoffman, personal communication), and that it thus held the promise of programmatic tie-ins with potential Norton Center events.

An additional motivation for the Norton Center to become involved in presenting Danville’s urban renewal story was that the Norton Center space, located at the corner of Walnut and College streets adjacent to the east side of Centre’s campus, is directly related to this history. The Norton Center, designed by the Frank Lloyd Wright Foundation’s William Wesley Peters, was built in 1973 and occupies the space that had housed Danville High School, Danville’s white high school before racial integration. The current site of Danville High School after racial integration, several blocks away to the northeast on Lexington Avenue, had been the site of the Kentucky College for Women, which integrated campuses with Centre College in 1962 after Centre completed construction of three new dormitories for women on the north side of Main St. as the very first phase of Danville’s first urban renewal project (racial school integration in the Danville Independent School system was officially mandated as of the 1964-65 school year when the new school building on Lexington Avenue was completed, but Black students had had the option to attend Danville High School for several years previous (“The History of Integration in Danville Schools” 2019). Thus, Danville’s first urban renewal project, which eventually affected Black families on Seventh and Rowe streets adjacent to the North side of Centre’s campus, was in part motivated by the land swap that gave Centre the space to build its new performing arts center.

The resulting exhibition in the Norton Center was titled “We Were Here: The African American Experience in Boyle County” (WWH). It consisted of large-format archival pictures, explanatory text, a large-format version of the virtual timeline of the urban renewal period created by Centre students, listening stations with excerpts from the Black oral history collection curated by Centre students, and displays of artifacts from the DBCAAHS collection. The pictures and format of WWH were based on the recent book that DBCAAHS published, titled African Americans in Boyle County. The first author also contributed essays for the Norton Center’s fall and spring programs that contextualized urban renewal in Danville and its effects on the Black community, as well as student contributions and reactions to the project.

A final notable effect of the Norton Center partnership with DBCAAHS were the opportunities it afforded to invite the African American community into a space from which many had previously felt excluded. The Norton Center hosted both an opening reception for WWH in Fall 2022 (“Norton Center debuts ‘We Were Here’ Exhibit” 2022) and a tenth anniversary celebration for DBCAAHS in Spring 2023 that foregrounded WWH. Members of the local Black community, including all of those who had provided oral history interviews, were invited to these events. The WWH exhibition also served as the basis for themed programming at the Norton Center during Fall 2022: Makin’ Cake with Wisconsin poet laureate Dasha Kelly Hamilton (also including “Affrilachian” poet and Danville native Frank X Walker and Kentucky Poet Laureate Crystal Wilkinson) , which focused on the theme of Black history and storytelling through the medium of food, and the opera concert given by Eric Owens of the Curtis Institute of Music (The tie-in with Eric Owens is that Robert Todd Duncan, the original actor cast in the role of Porgy in the George and Ira Gershwin’s landmark 1935 African-American opera Porgy and Bess, was a Danville native. Duncan played the role of Porgy more than 1,800 times. Eric Owens reprised the role of Porgy in a production of Porgy and Bess at New York’s Metropolitan Opera in 2019. Tickets for these events were made available free to DBCAAHS, which distributed them to community stakeholders.

Recreation: baseball, ballparks, and the Terrell Drive pool

From the exhibition panel texts by the We Were Here project team:

Before desegregation and urban renewal, Danville’s Black community supported a lively network of clubs, pool halls, restaurants, and two popular ballparks, all of which were important social sites. Recollections of these places became a recurring theme in the oral histories that students collected with DBCAAHS.

In the first part of the 20th century, baseball was an incredibly popular sport across the country, and Danville’s African American community has a robust history with the game. Beginning in the late 1800s, Danville had two fields where teams played amateur baseball at a very high level, notably the Danville Yankees and the Danville Cubs. Nearby Junction City also had a team in the 1920s, coached by World War I veteran and “Harlem Hellfighter” Wallace Gaines. The Danville teams played other teams from all over central Kentucky and, at first, traveled to away games in a converted cattle trailer. Later, the Victory Bus Lines bus replaced the cattle trailer.

For spectators, baseball games were a social event, and crowds went out to Duncan Hill’s ballpark (variously named Davis Park, Sportsman’s Park, Foag’s Park, Old Fort, or Yankee’s Park) on summer weekends and holidays to take in the game, to picnic, and to socialize. This park also served as the home field for Bate School football games and track meets. While baseball eventually became less popular, the local park continued to be an important social space for Danville’s Black community for years to come.

Today, the park is no longer active, nor are the restaurants, pool halls, or clubs shown in this section’s photographs. But, many memories about time spent in these places are preserved in the oral histories presented here.

Another important recreation site for Danville’s Black community was the Terrell Drive Swimming Pool, which was located near Bate-Wood Homes. Prior to integration, African Americans were barred from swimming in the pool at Sunnyside Park, and instead would either have to swim in Harrington Lake, in Clark’s Run in South Danville, or travel 36 miles to the nearest pool in a Black park, which was at Douglas Park in Lexington. After the tragic drowning of two African American boys in Harrington Lake, Danville local Cecil Cohen wrote to the Advocate-Messenger calling for a safe place for African American children to swim. After garnering community support through massive fundraising efforts, the pool was eventually constructed and opened to the public the summer of 1960. The pool was only open until 1967, when poor construction, lack of maintenance, and persistent flooding from nearby Clark’s Run forced it to close. Sadly, in 1974, another young African American boy drowned accidentally in the now-defunct pool, and it was filled in.

Centre/Danville-Boyle County African American Historical Society Partnership

The above context for urban renewal in Danville during the second half of the 20th century was the motivation and context for a long-term partnership between several courses taught by this article’s first author and DBCAAHS. DBCAAHS was founded in 2013, and its institutional vision is to be “committed to the collection, preservation, accessibility, promotion, and communication of the history of the Danville-Boyle County African American community”. The motivation for founding DBCAAHS came from Mr. Michael Hughes, a nearly lifelong resident of Danville who has served as DBCAAHS President since its founding. The lack of contemporary local knowledge or of any kind of systematic accounting of either the urban renewal period, or the Black experience in Boyle County more generally, was a key reason to found DBCAAHS (Michael Hughes and Michael Denis, personal communication). Since 2013, Mr. Hughes has collected thousands of photographs from local Black families, which he regularly colorizes and posts to Facebook.

Each August since 2015 (excluding two years at the height of the Covid pandemic), DBCAAHS has held an annual marquee event, the “Soul of Second Street'’ festival. This gathering is held in Constitution Square with food and music to celebrate the legacy of Black community in Boyle County and Black entrepreneurship on Danville’s Second Street. For several years, DBCAAHS also held a concomitant Black History conference with keynote speakers such as “Affrilachian” poet and Danvillian Frank X Walker. In 2019, the first author was a new assistant professor at Centre College, looking for a way to make their impending introduction to cultural anthropology course more dynamic by linking student engagement to the local community. The first author met Mr. Hughes at the Black History conference.

Our first partnership in 2019 was modest in scale, but was strategically positioned to serve as a building block for generating a database of knowledge on local urban renewal. The Introduction to Cultural Anthropology course is centered on the theme of “placemaking,” which serves as a conduit for students to explore foundational ideas in cultural anthropology. Students first read Keith Basso’s ethnography Wisdom Sits in Places and consider the rather striking and (for the largely non-Indigenous Centre student body) distant-from-personal-experience example of Western Apache place naming as it pertains to values, myth, and identity. Students then consider placemaking self-reflexively in their own lives with a short paper, and then complete an ethnographic project with a partner assigned from the class in which they argue for ongoing processes of placemaking on Centre’s campus. Next, students go on to consider the idea of placemaking from the point of view of the ways in which power and control over a historical narrative, linked to hegemonic control over location, time, and culture, also serves to normalize the exclusion of groups, identities, and counter-narratives. First, we consider the current identity of the Commonwealth of Kentucky, which to this day has a dominant, and also completely empirically untrue, narrative that there were never indigenous groups that lived permanently or in large numbers in any part of what is now Kentucky (Henderson 1992). To do so, we look at archaeological evidence of long-term construction, ritual use, and destruction of monumental architecture by indigenous groups within what is now the Commonwealth (e.g., Henry et al., 2021).

One of the final steps in this sequence themed on “placemaking” is to engage directly with DBCAAHS. The Introduction to Cultural Anthropology students meet with Mr. Hughes at Constitution Square Park during class time where he gives them a narrative tour, talking about the buildings and businesses that were there before the Second Street urban renewal project. Sites on the tour are a mural of famous and accomplished Black Danvillian William “Bunny” Davis, commissioned in 2018, and two Kentucky state historical markers that stand next to the Governor’s Circle walkway, one of which commemorates the South Second St. Black business district. The other commemorates a locally-famous instance in 1864 when hundreds of mostly enslaved Black men from Boyle County marched north to the US Army’s Camp Nelson to join the Union Army during the US Civil War. Mr. Hughes’ point with this exercise is to drive home the idea that these memorials are pale reflections of what was once on Second Street in terms of Black community, Black culture, and Black intergenerational wealth. Mr. Hughes then takes the students back to the DBCAAHS physical space, half a block away on North Second Street. DBCAAHS has occupied this space since July 2020, and was renovated by DBCAAHS members and other members of the local community. The space was provided by a local white family that has been long-time supporters of DBCAAHS’ efforts. Here, Mr. Hughes shows the students pictures and artifacts from Second Street and regional Black history, and narrates stories of his own and others’ experiences in Danville from the 1950s through the 1970s.

As a corollary to this experience, in 2019 the first author partnered with the Centre College library to put students’ first-hand experience hearing about the history of Second Street into conversation with the “official” narrative of the urban renewal period in Danville. In Fall 2019 and Fall 2020, the students were given search terms by Centre librarians (organizations, people, and buildings) relevant to the Second Street renewal project, and they then used newspapers.com to find associated articles published with reference to these search terms in the local newspaper, the Danville Advocate-Messenger. They then clipped the articles and posted them in chronological order using the application TimelineJS, creating an annotated, crowd-sourced chronology of Danville’s urban renewal period. Students then presented their findings in class. Starting in 2021, rather than adding to the timeline, students have been assigned to write a short reflection paper on the differences in the ways that information is presented in the “official” newspaper transcript during the renewal period (as presented in the timeline), versus the ways that they hear about Second Street from Mr. Hughes and other DBCAAHS members.

After initiating this partnership in 2019, we began a conversation about ways to increase the scale of student involvement in local Black history. At a Thanksgiving meal to which I was invited in November 2019 at Danville’s Saint James AME Church, we hit on the idea of capacitating students to conduct oral history interviews with members of the local Black community who had been affected by urban renewal. At that time, DBCAAHS had conducted some sporadic recorded interviews, but lacked the time, hardware, and person power to interview people systematically. The eventual outcome of this effort was the creation of the course “Lived Histories,” which the first author has taught twice during Centre’s three-week January term, in 2021 and 2023. This course focuses thematically on situating urban renewal, both locally and nationally, in the longue durée of African American History in the United States.

Methodologically and in terms of student engagement, the course capacitates students to conduct semi-structured life history interviews, which they conduct in small groups with older members of Danville’s Black community. Students also leave the course understanding the complex “back end” work involved in professional archiving, including the idea of informed consent and the associated consent and release documents, the role of the Institutional Review Board in overseeing research, the public nature of oral history interviewing and the limitations this poses on the kind of information that narrators will provide, creating transcripts and public access to the interviews, and the complexities of interpreting data that is by its nature imperfect and subjective. These interviews are recorded with podcasting equipment we purchased with Innovative Teaching Funds from Centre’s Center for Teaching and Learning.

During spring 2022, the first author was able to integrate the work they began in 2021 by participating in a grant funded by the Associated Colleges of the South (titled “Participatory History and Archiving: An Initiative to Promote and Support Undergraduate Instruction and Project Outcomes in Community-Based Research and Archiving”). This grant allowed the first author to integrate aspects of the digital archiving/oral history student research project into their course in linguistic anthropology, (titled “Language, Culture, and Power”). In spring 2022, students wrote analytical, language-focused papers on local oral histories of the urban renewal period that they also collected and archived. In a more recent iteration of this course, students had the option of participating in the oral history project or to complete an independent language-focused project.

During the Spring 2024 semester the first author had the opportunity to integrate the oral history project into Centre’s Anthropology Senior Seminar (ANT 500, the senior capstone course for majors). The theme of the senior seminar is “public anthropology,” which created a venue to work on a collaborative public-facing project linked to Black oral history. During the summer of 2022, in the leadup to our public exhibition (see below), the first author, a collaborator at Centre’s Norton Centre for the Arts, three senior members of DBCAAHS, and two student research assistants recorded six approximately two-hour sessions during which DBCAAHS partners narrated all of the photographs that we had chosen for public exhibition. In order to generate a permanent cloud-based version of the public exhibition, students in ANT 500 learned to use the audio editing software Audacity and the video editing software iMovie to create short movies that paired the archival photos with these narrations.

Integrating the oral history and local Black history project into this suite of courses – Anthropology 110, 347, 389, and 500 – was an attempt to create a flexible, productive, long-term, open-ended, and reciprocal community-based partnership with DBCAAHS. As of the Spring 2024 semester, Centre students have conducted 32 oral history interviews with local Black residents. Over 300 Centre students have been involved in the project in some way, and many anthropology majors have been involved in multiple aspects of the project over the course of their undergraduate careers. Groups of Centre students, and members of DBCAAHS have presented findings from the project at several regional and national history and anthropology conferences since 2022. DBCAAHS has supported four student interns since 2020, and the project has also supported four students for summer research assistantships over the summers of 2022, 2023, and 2024. The project maintains, through Centre College’s Grace Doherty Library, a permanent site providing public access to all of the digital materials we have generated, including all of the long-form oral histories, audio clips of the oral histories created for the listening stations in the public exhibition (see below), the final timeline from the urban renewal period using articles from the Danville Advocate-Messenger, and archival photos and narrations from the public exhibition. The Centre library also hosts the “official” archival materials on servers that it maintains and pays for in “digital deep freeze.”
