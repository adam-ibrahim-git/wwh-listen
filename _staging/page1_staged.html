About the project

In the South, many Black communities flourished after the Civil War, as formerly enslaved people became able to acquire land for the first time. Boyle County alone had sixteen such communities during the decades following the war. At the same time that Black people in the South gained some rights as citizens, however, racial segregation in schooling, housing, business, and even recreation was also enforced both informally through everyday conventions and formally through legislation. Some of the most consequential legislation did not even, on its surface, deal with race.

Following World War II, the United States government implemented the Housing Act of 1949. Between 1949 and 1973, over two-thousand “urban renewal” projects were carried out across the country with federal support. The Act was intended to identify “slums and blighted areas” for “clearance” and “redevelopment.” In much of the South, these projects targeted Black communities that had operated separately from white society for decades, communities often labeled “blighted” because their entrepreneurs and homeowners had been denied access to financial capital and credit. This was the case in Danville, where condemnation suits were filed against Black property owners when the local Urban Renewal Agency could not negotiate a direct sale.

The result was that the historically Black parts of town were splintered. As Charles Grey, Danville native and Vice-President of the Danville-Boyle County African-American Historical Society puts it, “they took the dirt, dirt we’ll never get back. The only thing we have now is the history and the memories.”

The We Were Here exhibition aims to lay bare, from the perspective of Danville’s African American community, the consequences of urban renewal carried out in our own Central Kentucky community. Through photographs and oral histories, it also preserves and represents our region’s vibrant Black history. This website is the permanent archive of the exhibition.

A word from Michael Hughes

(President of the Danville-Boyle County African-American Historical Society)

Stories like those in this exhibition could be told all across America. But these stories are ours. The stories of hardship, rejection, celebration, and triumph you will see here were lived in our communities, our neighborhoods. You will see the once-vibrant Second Street Business District that was our social hub. Hundreds of people each week bustled through its restaurants, beauty shops, barber shops, pool halls, doctor offices, dentist offices, and the Masonic Lodge Hall which stood tall in the middle of the strip as a beacon of our heritage. You will see the many proud, but now mostly forgotten, Black neighborhoods that were home to generations of Boyle County citizens. We remember a time when there were no locks on the doors, when a knock was answered by a “come-on in” and sharing what you had. We Were Here.

Systemic racism in the form of urban renewal destroyed a culture almost 100 years in the making. In two to three years, it was all gone. Sharing this long-gone way of life is our mission at the Danville-Boyle County African-American Historical Society. It is our mission not only to tell the story, but, through our best efforts, to share the lives of folks whose voices are no longer heard, whose names are the only record of its existence. We are proud to share our history through this collaboration with Centre College and the Norton Center.

A word from the Norton Center for the Arts

In 2021, the Norton Center was approached by Centre College faculty and members of the Danville-Boyle County African American Historical Society to consider installing a display that connected to the DBCAAHS’s ongoing work to collect oral histories and historic photographs from Boyle County’s African American community.

After being presented with such an amazing opportunity, the Norton Center’s first priority was to ensure that a display about history also forefronted the arts as a vehicle for learning and engagement. It soon became clear that the essence of these oral histories and photographs is stories, and the art of storytelling is something the Norton Center has always valued as an important artform. Thus, it was ultimately an easy answer to collaborate in this newly formed partnership and, before long, the concept of a display grew into a larger, multi-media exhibition that would use the entirety of the Norton Center’s gallery space. Of course, the Norton Center also wanted to make even more connections with this project. So, in addition to the exhibition, several programs for the upcoming 2022-2023 Norton Center performing arts series make direct, and sometimes unexpected correlations to the topics the exhibition explores.

This project brings together the work of many different community organizations, campus departments and individuals in order to make publicly accessible a collection of meaningful and highly relevant multi-genre and multi-generational experiences. Through the course of this project’s collaborative planning and implementation, these different groups of people have met and learned from each other. In the case of the Norton Center, we now consider these partners friends and even family.

What makes this opportunity even more significant for the Norton Center is how directly the experience of working on this project connects to the larger goals and vision for our institution and those of Centre College at large. It has been an honor to be part of an organic, community-led partnership that has afforded the Norton Center the opportunity to reflect on our Statement of Solidarity, specifically with regard to using institutional spaces to represent themes of diversity, equity and inclusion, and social justice.

Life is short, but memories can last for an eternity if they are preserved, archived and presented in transparent and accessible ways. Storytelling, in any form, remains an invaluable way to ensure memories and history - and the lessons we can learn from them - are not forgotten.

Using this site

We recommend beginning with The Exhibition, whose first section provides historical grounding for everything else. From there, explore the Voices of community members, walk the exhibition, or find places on the Map. Most oral history clips include a transcript on the same page.

A note on historical language: this archive includes content containing historical language that reflects the society and time period in which it was produced. Documents, images, publications, and other materials are preserved in their original state and context, and do not reflect the views of the project or of Centre College.

Exhibition acknowledgements

The We Were Here Project, which includes the “We Were Here”: The African American Experience in Boyle County exhibition, the Makin’ Cake and Eric Owens & Curtis Opera Singers performances, and the many additional related engagement activities and programs, is truly a collaborative community partnership that uses storytelling to share a crucial part of our community’s history and culture.

So many incredible collaborators and partners from within the Centre College and Boyle County communities jumped at the opportunity to be part of this project, and for that we are very grateful. From the perspective of our Commonwealth, there was also significant support from government and non-profit organizations and commercial businesses to ensure that the stories, images, artifacts and experiential learning opportunities connected to this project are heard and accessible to all.

About the project title

The title “We Were Here” comes from the play Good Blues Tonight!, which was written by Danville native, Robby Henson. The play, which revolves around Danville’s Second Street in the 1950s, debuted at the Pioneer Playhouse in 2016 and was re-staged at Lexington’s Lyric Theatre in 2017.
